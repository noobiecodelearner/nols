Recommended Reading

These texts have shaped how I think about learning systems—not as isolated models, but as entities that evolve, fail, and interact with the world around them.

Learning as a System (Not an Algorithm)

Herbert A. Simon — The Sciences of the Artificial → Intelligence as design under constraints, not optimization in a vacuum.
Norbert Wiener — Cybernetics → Feedback, control, and communication as the substrate of learning systems.
Judea Pearl — The Book of Why → Causality as a missing layer in purely statistical learning.

Failure, Generalization, and Distribution Shift

D. Sculley et al. — Hidden Technical Debt in Machine Learning Systems → Most failures don't come from models, but from the systems wrapped around them.
Benjamin Recht et al. — Do ImageNet Classifiers Generalize? → Benchmark success can mask brittleness in the real world.
Martin Arjovsky et al. — Invariant Risk Minimization → Generalization requires structure, not just data.

Representation, Geometry, and Structure

Mikhail Belkin et al. — Reconciling Modern Machine Learning and the Bias–Variance Tradeoff → Overparameterization changes how we should think about capacity and error.
Michael Bronstein et al. — Geometric Deep Learning → Inductive bias as geometry, not heuristics.
Yoshua Bengio et al. — Representation Learning: A Review → Good representations are the real engine of learning.

Humans, Infrastructure, and Feedback Loops

Selbst et al. — Fairness and Abstraction in Sociotechnical Systems → ML failures often arise from abstraction boundaries, not intent.
Saleema Amershi et al. — Guidelines for Human–AI Interaction → Model behavior changes once humans enter the loop.
Langdon Winner — Do Artifacts Have Politics? → Technical systems encode values, whether we notice or not.

Evaluation, Metrics, and What We Choose to Measure

Amodei et al. — Concrete Problems in AI Safety → Misaligned objectives reveal themselves only at scale.
Lipton & Steinhardt — Troubling Trends in Machine Learning Scholarship → Benchmark chasing can distort scientific progress.
Hand — Measurement Theory and Practice → What we measure determines what we think is improving.

Writing, Clarity, and Thinking Well (Meta)

Richard Feynman — Cargo Cult Science → The appearance of rigor is not rigor.
George Orwell — Politics and the English Language → Clear thinking begins with clear writing.
Paul Graham — Essays (selected) → Writing as a tool for discovering what you actually believe.

This list is not exhaustive. It evolves as my thinking does.
